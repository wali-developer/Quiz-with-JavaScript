Question 2

At diagnosis James had an HbA1c of 67mmol/mol. After 3 months of making changes to his diet and activity levels his HbA1c is now 52mmol/mol. At what stage does NICE recommend that medication should be started?

Correct

NICE guidelines state that medication will be needed to treat an HbA1c of over 48mmol/mol (6.5%) if lifestyle interventions alone are inadequate.1

The figure of 53mmol/mol (7.0%) is the target recommended for people being treated with a single drug with an associated isk of hypoglycaemia. The figure of 58mmol/mol (>7.5%) is the level at which an intensification of therapy should be considered – i.e. reinforce lifestyle advice and consider an additional drug to improve blood glucose control. Guidelines suggest treatment goals that consider patients’ specific circumstances.1-3

Question 3

Hannah is 73 years old, and has had type 2 diabetes for a number of years, during which her blood glucose has been well controlled. She is being treated with metformin 1g bd but over the past 6 months her HbA1c has crept up, and is currently 59mmol/mol.
Hannah manages well in general although she lives alone and her only daughter lives 80 miles away. Which of the following would NOT be appropriate?





Incorrect

Please select another option

Correct

It would be entirely appropriate to ask Hannah about how and when she takes her medication in order to assess her adherence. It would also be right to ask her about how she feels about adding in more medication to reach a lower target or whether she is happier with a slightly higher target than might be recommended in someone younger, in order to avoid any possible side effects. It would not be appropriate to lecture Hannah or suggest that the rise in her HbA1c levels is her ‘fault’ as this could lead to a breakdown in the therapeutic relationship.1 A glycaemic target of 59mmol/mol is appropriate for ‘biologically young’ older adults.2 Very few people with diabetes, aged 70 years or older, would be anticipated to benefit from intensive intervention to targets below 53mmol/mol (7.0%).2 Care must be taken when adding in therapies, as the consequences of hypoglycaemia may be just as severe in the fit as the frail older adult.2

Question 4

Hannah is taking her metformin as prescribed and has not noticed any changes in her diet or activity levels that might explain the rise in her HbA1c over the past 6 months. Her current BMI is 26.8kg/m2 and this has been stable for the past few years, although it has been higher in the past. Hannah tries to keep her weight down as much as she can. Her HbA1c is 59mmol/mol, just above the threshold NICE recommends for intensification of therapy. Her most recent eGFR is 54ml/min/1.73m2, indicating mild renal impairment. Hannah is keen to aim for a tighter target for her HbA1c and is happy to take another oral hypoglycaemic agent in order to achieve this. Which of these options is LESS appropriate for Hannah?





Incorrect

Please select another option

Correct

Sulfonylureas can cause weight gain and hypoglycaemia,1 and as Hannah works hard at keeping her weight under control they would not be an ideal choice for this patient. Furthermore, because she lives alone it would be preferable to avoid any drug that might increase the risk of hypoglycaemia. Pioglitazone has a low risk of hypoglycaemia but is associated with weight gain.1 Pioglitazone is also associated with an increased risk of heart failure, bladder cancer and bone fracture, and risk factors for these conditions – including increased age – should be carefully evaluated before treatment.2 DPP-4 inhibitors have been used in frail and elderly patients, and have similar efficacy in older patients to that in younger populations.3 In patients with renal impairment, a DPP-4 inhibitor that doesn’t require dose adjustment, (for example, linagliptin [Trajenta®]) might be a suitable option.4 If Hannah’s kidney function was not reduced, an alternative would be an SGLT2 inhibitor, which works by preventing reabsorption of glucose in the kidneys. This leads to increased excretion of glucose in the urine, (averaging approximately 78 g/day) resulting in an immediate reduction in blood glucose levels. However, the majority of SGLT2 inhibitors are not indicated in patients with an eGFR 60ml /min/1.73m2.5
